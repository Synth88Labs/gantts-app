Recruitment and Hiring Plan Template

A free recruitment and hiring plan template covering a single requisition end to end — role definition and headcount approval, sourcing, screening, the interview loop, assessment and references, offer and negotiation, the candidate's notice period, and the start date. What's included

Most hiring plans underestimate the same two things: how long approval takes at the front, and how long notice takes at the back. The template gives both real bars so the start date is honest:

  • Role definition & approval — Business case, job description, level and salary banding, headcount approval and requisition opening. Milestone: requisition approved.
  • Sourcing — Job posting, referral push, direct sourcing and outreach, agency briefing if used, and pipeline build to a target number of candidates.
  • Screening — Application review, recruiter screens, hiring-manager screens and the shortlist decision. Milestone: shortlist agreed.
  • Interview loop — Structured interviews across the panel, technical or functional exercise, scheduling logistics and debrief sessions.
  • Assessment & decision — Work sample or case review, final panel, scorecard consolidation, references and background checks. Milestone: hiring decision.
  • Offer & negotiation — Compensation approval, verbal offer, written contract, negotiation and signature. Milestone: offer accepted.
  • Notice period & pre-start — Candidate resignation and notice, keep-warm contact, equipment and access setup, and day one. Milestone: start date.
The notice period is not admin — it is often the longest single bar on the chart. A senior hire on three months' notice means the requisition you approve today produces a working person five or six months from now. Plans that stop at "offer accepted" consistently promise capacity a quarter before it exists.

How to customize it

  1. Work backwards from when you need the person productive, not from when you want them to sign.
  2. Set the notice bar to the real market norm for the role and country — one month, three months and six months produce very different plans.
  3. Duplicate the sourcing through offer phases per role if you are hiring a team, and stagger them against interviewer capacity.
  4. Add an agency or search-firm track with its own milestones if you are using one; the briefing and shortlist dates are contractual.
  5. Insert visa or right-to-work steps as their own tasks where relevant — they can exceed the notice period.
  6. Keep the keep-warm tasks on the chart. Between signature and start is when accepted candidates get counter-offered away.

Scheduling tips

  • Get headcount approved before you post. A requisition that reaches final panel without budget sign-off wastes the candidate's time and your credibility.
  • Book the interview panel's time in advance. Scheduling, not decision-making, is the largest source of delay in most hiring loops.
  • Compress the loop, not the screening. Strong candidates are usually in more than one process; a loop that runs over three weeks loses them to whoever moved faster.
  • Start references before the final decision where you can. They routinely take a week of chasing and sit directly between decision and offer.
  • Treat the offer as a negotiation with a duration. Building in a few days for a counter and a response is more realistic than assuming same-day acceptance.
  • Hand over to onboarding at signature, not at day one. Equipment, accounts and the first-week plan all need lead time inside the notice period.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to hire someone?

Commonly 6–12 weeks from approved requisition to signed offer, plus the notice period on top. This template runs about five months from business case to start date, which is realistic for a role with a one to three month notice.

How does this fit with the onboarding template?

This plan ends at the start date; the employee onboarding template picks up from day one through the first ninety days. Run them back to back for the full picture.

What is the most common cause of a slow hiring process?

Interview scheduling and slow internal decisions, not sourcing. That is why the interview loop and debrief are separate tasks here rather than one block.

Can I use this to plan several hires at once?

Yes — duplicate the sourcing-to-offer phases per role and stagger the interview loops so you do not book the same panel twice in the same week.
