Warehouse Setup Project Plan Template

What's included

A warehouse project is three projects sharing a building: the physical fit-out, the system integration, and the inventory move. They have different lead times and different failure modes, so the template runs them as separate tracks:

  • Requirements & network study — Volume and SKU profiling, throughput modelling, network and location analysis, and the capital case. Milestone: business case approved.
  • Site selection & lease — Site search and shortlist, building survey, floor loading and power checks, heads of terms, lease negotiation and landlord works. Milestone: lease signed.
  • Layout & racking — Storage strategy and slotting design, racking layout and permits, fire and sprinkler design, racking manufacture and installation, mezzanine and pick modules.
  • WMS selection & integration — Requirements and vendor selection, configuration, ERP and carrier integrations, data migration, and user acceptance testing. Milestone: WMS UAT signed off.
  • MHE & equipment — Forklift and reach truck procurement, conveyor and sortation where used, packing benches, scanners, print and apply, and charging infrastructure.
  • Fit-out & infrastructure — Power and lighting, network and wireless survey, dock levellers and doors, office and welfare, security and access control.
  • Recruitment & training — Site management hires, operative recruitment, WMS and equipment training, and licensed operator certification.
  • Inventory transfer & go-live — Stock cleanse, transfer waves, cycle counting, parallel running, cutover and the volume ramp. Milestone: full volume.
The wireless survey is a dependency, not a detail. Every scan-driven process in the building assumes coverage in every aisle at full rack height, and racking changes the RF environment completely. Survey after racking is installed and before go-live, with time to add access points — teams that survey an empty shed discover the dead aisles during their first peak.

How to customize it

  1. Anchor the plan to the date you must exit the current site or hit a customer commitment; that fixes go-live and everything works backwards.
  2. Adjust racking manufacture lead time to your quoted figure — it is frequently the longest single item after the lease.
  3. Add a row per integration (ERP, carriers, marketplace, returns) once the WMS vendor is chosen; each has its own test cycle.
  4. Split the inventory transfer into waves by SKU velocity so fast movers land first and the site can trade while the tail arrives.
  5. Extend the parallel running bar if you cannot afford a hard cutover; running both sites costs money but protects service.
  6. Mark lease signature, racking complete, WMS UAT sign-off, first order shipped and full volume as milestones.

Scheduling tips

  • Check floor loading and power before heads of terms. A building that cannot take your racking loads or your charging load is not a cheaper building, it is the wrong one.
  • Choose the WMS before the racking layout is frozen. Slotting, location naming and pick-path logic all follow the system, and retrofitting them into installed racking is expensive.
  • Order MHE against real lead times. Forklift and reach truck delivery has stretched to many months in recent years; the order date belongs on the chart.
  • Ramp, do not switch. Plan the first weeks at a deliberately reduced volume so process problems surface while there is still capacity to absorb them.
  • Cleanse stock before you move it. Transferring obsolete inventory costs money twice — once to move it and again to store it.
  • Baseline at lease signature. Before that everything is optional; after it, rent is running and every week of slippage has a number.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to set up a new warehouse?

Commonly 9–15 months from business case to go-live for a mid-sized distribution centre, driven mainly by site acquisition, racking lead time and WMS integration. The template uses a twelve-month schedule.

What is usually the longest lead time?

Racking manufacture and materials handling equipment, both of which are now measured in months. The lease itself often runs longer than either, which is why site selection starts the plan.

Should the WMS go live at the same time as the building?

Not necessarily. Many sites run the new building on the existing system first, then cut the system over separately, so only one variable changes at a time. The template keeps the tracks separate so you can choose.

How do I plan the inventory transfer?

In waves by SKU velocity, with cycle counting after each wave and a parallel running period before the old site closes. Those rows are pre-loaded in the final phase.
