Medical Clinic Opening Timeline Template

A free medical clinic opening timeline template built around the three clocks that decide when you can actually see a patient — licensing, inspection and clinician credentialing. What's included

A clinic that is built, staffed and equipped still cannot treat anyone until three separate approvals land. The template treats those as the spine rather than the tail:

  • Business case, site & licensing pathway — Service line definition and demand analysis, site selection and lease, confirming the licensing pathway with the health authority, and the entity, NPI and tax registrations that everything downstream needs. Milestones: lease signed, licensing pathway confirmed.
  • Clinician credentialing & payer enrolment — Physician recruitment, primary source verification, medical staff committee review, state licence and DEA registration, and payer enrolment — a long clock run by other organisations. Milestones: clinicians credentialed, payer contracts effective.
  • Design & regulated fit-out — Medical planning room by room, construction documents and health authority plan review, permit, rough-in, medical gas piping, HVAC pressure relationships and infection-control barriers, and cleanable finishes. Milestone: fit-out substantially complete.
  • Equipment, IT & commissioning — Capital equipment purchase orders, imaging delivery and installation, medical gas verification, biomedical commissioning and calibration, the physicist survey, and the EHR build and interfaces. Milestone: equipment commissioned and calibrated.
  • Inspections, licensing & accreditation — Fire marshal and occupancy, infection control risk assessment close-out, the health department clinic licensing inspection, CLIA for the in-house lab, and accreditation readiness. Milestone: clinic licence issued.
  • Staffing, dry run & go-live — Clinical and front-desk hiring, orientation and competency sign-off, supply chain and sterile stock, a mock patient day, test claims through the clearinghouse, and referring physician outreach. Milestones: first patient seen, full schedule open.
You cannot see a patient until licensing, inspection and credentialing have all landed — and credentialing is the one you control least. Primary source verification, medical staff committee review, state licensure, DEA registration and payer enrolment each run on someone else's queue, and 90 to 180 days from a complete file is normal. Start it the day you make an offer, not when the building is finished, because a fully fitted clinic with uncredentialed clinicians and no active payer contracts is an empty building that is also losing money.

How to customize it

  1. Start the credentialing bar at the offer date and give it your payers' real turnaround, not the best case. If it finishes after the licence, credentialing is your critical path and the chart should say so.
  2. Add one row per clinician if you are opening with several. They do not all clear at the same time, and the opening date depends on the slowest one, not the average.
  3. Separate payer enrolment from credentialing. Being privileged to practise and being contracted to bill are different processes with different clocks, and the second one determines revenue, not access.
  4. Keep medical gas piping, verification and certification as their own bars. It is a specialist installation with a third-party verifier and it cannot be absorbed into general mechanical works.
  5. Add the infection control risk assessment as a real activity with a close-out, especially if you are fitting out inside an occupied building.
  6. Put the mock patient day in before go-live and staff it fully. It is the only rehearsal you get of check-in, rooming, the EHR, the equipment and the claim, all in one flow.

Scheduling tips

  • Confirm the licensing pathway before you sign the lease. Whether you need a certificate of need, which licence class applies and what plan review requires can change the space, the build cost and the timeline entirely.
  • Submit credentialing files complete or not at all. An incomplete file does not start the clock — it goes to the back of the queue when it is corrected, and that single administrative slip is the most common cause of a delayed opening.
  • Design for plan review, not just for the builder. Health authority plan review has specific requirements on room sizes, sinks, pressure relationships and finishes, and a redesign after a review comment costs far more than getting it right first.
  • Book the fire marshal and health department inspections early and expect a re-inspection. Their diaries are not yours, and scheduling a go-live that assumes a first-time pass on both is a bet you will usually lose.
  • Commission and calibrate equipment with the biomed team, not the vendor alone. Delivery and installation are not the same as being calibrated, surveyed and recorded on an asset register the inspector will ask to see.
  • Run test claims through the clearinghouse before the first real patient. Discovering a broken payer configuration in week one means seeing patients you cannot bill for, which is worse than opening a week later.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to open a medical clinic?

Twelve to sixteen months is typical for a fitted-out clinic in leased space, which is roughly what the template uses. The build is rarely the constraint; licensing and credentialing are.

How long does clinician credentialing take?

Commonly 90–180 days from a complete file, and longer if payer enrolment is included. It is run by verification organisations, medical staff committees, state boards and payers, so it should be started at the offer date and tracked as a long-lead item.

What makes a medical fit-out different from a commercial one?

Medical gas piping with third-party verification, HVAC pressure relationships between clean and soiled areas, infection-control barriers during construction, cleanable finishes and shielding for imaging rooms — none of which appear in ordinary office fit-out.

Can we open before payer contracts are effective?

Physically yes, financially no. Seeing patients you cannot bill for is a common and expensive mistake, so payer contracts effective is treated as its own milestone rather than being folded into credentialing.

What inspections are needed before opening?

Typically fire marshal and building occupancy, a health department clinic licensing inspection, medical gas verification, a radiation physicist survey where imaging is installed, and CLIA certification for any in-house laboratory.
