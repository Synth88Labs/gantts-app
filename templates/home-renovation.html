What's included

The template is organized into the phases of a typical remodel, each pre-filled with common tasks and inspection milestones so you can adapt a real renovation timeline rather than start from a blank page:

  • Planning — design and budget, then permits and approvals. Milestone: permits approved.
  • Demolition — strip-out, disposal and site prep for the trades.
  • Structural & rough-in — framing, then electrical, plumbing and HVAC rough-in. Milestone: rough-in inspection passed.
  • Finishes — drywall, paint, flooring and the final inspection. Milestone: project complete.

The renovation phases explained

A remodel is a chain of dependencies, and getting the order wrong is expensive. Planning locks the design and — critically — the permits, because nothing legitimate starts before they're approved. Demolition clears the way. Structural and rough-in is where framing goes up and the electrical, plumbing and HVAC get run inside the walls, all of which must pass inspection before anything gets closed up. Only then do finishes go on. Seeing it as a Gantt makes the sequence obvious and stops trades from being booked out of order.

Permits, contractors & inspections

Three things derail renovations most: permits that arrive late, trades that overlap badly, and inspections that fail and force rework. This template puts permits at the front of the timeline, gives each trade its own bar with an owner so contractors don't collide, and treats every inspection as a milestone gate. When you open it in the editor you can link dependencies so a failed rough-in inspection visibly pushes drywall and everything after it — no guessing about the new finish date.

Renovation planning tips

  • Get permits early. They're the most common cause of a delayed start — put them first and add buffer.
  • Order long-lead items up front. Custom cabinets, windows and tile can take weeks; schedule them during planning so they don't stall finishes.
  • Treat inspections as hard gates. Don't let a trade start work that a failed inspection would force you to redo.
  • Build in contingency. Renovations uncover surprises behind walls — add float so one discovery doesn't blow the whole schedule.
