What's included

The template is organized into the four phases of a typical launch, each pre-filled with common tasks and milestones so you can adapt rather than start from a blank page:

  • Strategy — market and competitor research, audience definition, positioning and messaging. Milestone: positioning signed off.
  • Product readiness — feature finalization, beta / early-access program, bug triage and pricing & packaging. Milestone: release candidate ready.
  • Go-to-market — website and creative assets, PR and email campaigns, content and demand generation, and sales enablement. Milestone: GTM assets approved.
  • Launch — pre-launch checklist, launch day, and post-launch review. Milestones: launch day, post-launch retro.
The go-to-market timeline is one chain. PR can't go out before messaging is locked; sales enablement waits on final pricing. The launch phases explained

Strategy is where you decide who you're for and why you win. Market research and positioning feed everything downstream, so they sit at the front of the go-to-market timeline. Product readiness runs in parallel: your beta and pricing work must converge on a release candidate before the noise of launch begins. Go-to-market is the build-out — assets, PR, email, content and the sales materials that let the team sell on day one. Finally, launch compresses everything into a pre-launch checklist, a hard launch-day milestone, and a post-launch review that captures what to repeat next time.

Product launch planning tips

  • Work backward from launch day. Fix the date first, then place the pre-launch checklist, PR embargo and asset deadlines ahead of it with real buffer.
  • Protect the critical path. The chain from positioning → assets → PR usually drives the launch date; a slip there slips the whole launch.
  • Run product and GTM in parallel. Don't wait for a finished product to start marketing — overlap the tracks and gate the risky handoffs as milestones.
  • Enable sales before, not after. Demos, pricing sheets and battlecards should be ready before launch day, not scrambled together the morning of.
  • Book the post-launch review. Put the retro on the timeline now so lessons from this launch improve the next product launch plan.
