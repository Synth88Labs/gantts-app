What's included

Apparel schedules are built backwards from the delivery window, and the sample rounds are what consume the runway. The template follows the standard T&A sequence:

  • Concept & design — Range planning, design finalization, fabric and trim selection, and the tech pack that everything downstream depends on.
  • Sampling — Proto sample, fit sample and pre-production (PP) sample, each with a comment-and-revise loop. Milestone: PP sample approved.
  • Materials — Fabric booking, lab dips and strike-offs, trim ordering and in-house inspection. Milestone: fabric in-house.
  • Pre-production — Marker making, pattern grading, size set and the PP meeting that releases the line.
  • Bulk production — Cutting, sewing, in-line QC, finishing and pressing.
  • Finishing & shipment — Final inspection, packing, ex-factory and transit to the delivery window. Milestone: ex-factory.
PP sample approval is the gate the whole calendar turns on. Bulk cutting should not start before it, and every day the approval slips is a day removed from production — not from the delivery date, which the buyer has already fixed. Show it as a milestone with cutting dependent on it.

How to customize it

  1. Set the delivery date and work backwards; apparel calendars are countback calendars.
  2. Adjust sample rounds — some buyers require additional fit rounds, each adding two to three weeks.
  3. Extend fabric lead time for mill-dyed or specialty fabrics, which dominate the early calendar.
  4. Add rows per style or per colourway if you are running a multi-style drop.
  5. Include a shipping mode row; sea freight adds weeks that must sit inside the calendar.

Scheduling tips

  • Count back from ex-factory, not from delivery. Transit time is fixed and non-negotiable, so ex-factory is your real deadline.
  • Fabric is usually the long pole. Booking, dyeing and shipping fabric routinely takes longer than sewing the garment.
  • Build in buyer comment time. Sample approvals wait on someone else's calendar; two weeks per round is realistic.
  • Protect the size set. Skipping it to save days almost always costs more in bulk re-work.
  • Track by style. A drop is only as ready as its slowest style, which an aggregate bar hides.

Frequently asked questions

What is a time and action (T&A) calendar?

The apparel industry term for a production schedule that counts backwards from the delivery date through ex-factory, production, sampling and materials. This template is a T&A calendar in Gantt form.

How long before delivery should an apparel calendar start?

Commonly 4–6 months for a standard order, longer for mill-dyed fabric or complex construction. Sampling and fabric lead time consume most of that runway.

What usually delays a garment order?

Fabric lead time and sample approval rounds, far more often than sewing capacity. Both get their own tracked phases in the template.
