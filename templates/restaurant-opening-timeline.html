What's included

Opening a restaurant is a permit project with a kitchen attached. The template puts licensing on its own track, because that is where most opening dates are actually lost:

  • Concept & lease — Concept and menu direction, site search, financial model, lease negotiation and signature. Milestone: lease signed.
  • Design & permits — Space planning, kitchen design, architectural drawings, permit submission and approval — the phase with the least predictable duration.
  • Build-out — Demolition, MEP rough-in, kitchen infrastructure, finishes, millwork, front-of-house fit-out and the hood and fire suppression install.
  • Equipment & smallwares — Kitchen equipment ordered against lead times, delivery and installation, plus POS, furniture, and smallwares.
  • Licensing & inspections — Business licence, food service permit, liquor licence, health and fire inspections and the certificate of occupancy. Milestone: cleared to open.
  • Hiring, menu & soft opening — Chef and management hires, line staff, menu costing and testing, staff training, friends-and-family service and the soft opening. Milestone: open.
Start the liquor licence the week you sign the lease. In many jurisdictions it takes longer than the entire build-out and cannot be accelerated with money or effort, so an opening date set without it is a guess. It belongs on the chart as an early, long, independent bar.

How to customize it

  1. Anchor the timeline to lease signature — rent is running from that date, which is what makes every downstream delay expensive.
  2. Extend the permit phase if you are in a jurisdiction with slow plan review; six to twelve weeks is common and not unusual.
  3. Set equipment orders against real quoted lead times, and place them before the build-out rows that depend on them.
  4. Add a row per inspection (health, fire, building, hood) — each is a gate, not a formality.
  5. Keep friends-and-family and soft opening as separate bars; they serve different purposes and both need staffing.

Scheduling tips

  • Permits and licences run in parallel, not in sequence. Submit everything as early as each authority will accept it, rather than waiting for the previous approval.
  • Order the hood early. Hood and fire suppression frequently gate both the build inspection and the kitchen finish work.
  • Hire the chef before the menu. Menu costing, equipment needs and training all depend on that hire, so it is a dependency rather than a staffing detail.
  • Protect the training window. Compressing training to protect an opening date is the fastest route to a bad opening month of reviews.
  • Track rent burn against the schedule. Every week of slippage after lease signature has a known cost — showing it next to the chart focuses decisions.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to open a restaurant?

Commonly 6–12 months from lease signature to opening, with permits and build-out taking the largest share. The template uses a nine-month schedule you can compress or extend.

What usually delays a restaurant opening?

Permit review and liquor licensing far more often than construction. Both are third-party durations, which is why the template puts licensing on its own parallel track from early on.

Can I use this for a café or a bar instead?

Yes. The structure is the same — drop the liquor rows for a café, or trim the kitchen build-out rows for a bar-led concept.
