What's included

A fab is not finished when the building is finished. It is finished when tools are qualified and wafers run, and everything between those two points is governed by a protocol. The template follows that transition:

  • Site, permits & base build — Seismic and vibration survey, environmental and hazardous-materials permits, the basis of design and the tool set freeze that everything else is sized from, long-lead utility equipment and the piling and waffle slab. Milestones: tool set frozen, permits granted.
  • Shell & cleanroom envelope — Structural steel and envelope, subfab and slab penetrations, dry-in, then the cleanroom wall and ceiling grid, raised access floor and fan filter units. Milestone: building dry-in complete.
  • Facility utilities & abatement — Ultrapure water plant and loop flush, bulk and specialty gas yard, valve manifold boxes and leak testing, exhaust and scrubbers, and substation energisation — all driven to the hook-up points, not to a generic "complete". Milestone: utilities available at hook-up.
  • Cleanroom certification & protocol changeover — The construction protocol changeover, gowning and airlocks, material entry procedures, air balancing and laminar flow, particle counts and ISO class certification, and the vibration and EMI survey. Milestone: cleanroom certified.
  • Tool move-in, hook-up & qualification — Rigging plan and delivery sequence, litho, etch, deposition and metrology waves, hook-up to gases, exhaust and UPW, vendor installation qualification and operational qualification. Milestone: tool qualification complete.
  • Process qualification & first silicon — MES and automation integration, recipe loading, first wafer through a single module, the full-flow pilot lot, yield learning and customer qualification. Milestones: first silicon out, volume production release.
There is one date on a fab programme that changes the rules for everyone: the cleanroom protocol changeover. Before it, this is a construction site with hard hats and welding. After it, every person is gowned, every material is wiped in through an airlock, and productivity per trade drops sharply. Plan the work either side of that line deliberately — anything that can be finished dirty must be finished before it, and any bar that crosses it needs a longer duration than the same work would take outside. Programmes that treat it as an ordinary handover lose months relearning it in the field.

How to customize it

  1. Freeze the tool set early and mark it as a milestone. Every utility size, every penetration and every hook-up drawing derives from it, and a late tool swap ripples through the whole facility design.
  2. Re-sequence the tool waves to your own utility readiness rather than to vendor delivery dates. A tool that lands before its gas and exhaust are live occupies floor space and qualifies nothing.
  3. Give ultrapure water its own flush and resistivity qualification bar after installation. The plant being built is not the same as the loop being clean enough to hook a tool to.
  4. Split installation qualification from operational qualification. The vendor proving the tool powers up is a different event, with different people, from your process team proving it holds an SPC baseline.
  5. Add explicit bars for the protocol changeover, gowning procedures and material entry. They are activities with durations, not a memo.
  6. Put the vibration, EMI and acoustic survey before litho tool move-in. Finding a vibration problem after a stepper is set is the most expensive discovery on this schedule.

Scheduling tips

  • Hook-up, not delivery, is the real milestone. Track tools by the date they are connected to live utilities and powered, because a warehouse full of delivered tools is worth nothing to the qualification schedule.
  • Build the utilities to a hook-up-point date. "UPW plant complete" and "UPW available at the litho bay hook-up point at spec resistivity" are months apart and only the second one unblocks a tool.
  • Protect the abatement schedule. Exhaust and scrubbers are unglamorous and routinely deprioritised, and no gas-using tool can be qualified without them — they quietly sit on the critical path to first silicon.
  • Plan the vibration survey around real conditions. A survey run before the fans and pumps are running does not represent the environment the litho tools will live in.
  • Do not schedule yield learning as a fixed bar. Defect density comes down on a curve that is partly experimental; give it a realistic window and a contingency rather than a date the business plan wants.
  • Get MES and automation integration started during tool install, not after. The recipe and material-handling integration work is software with its own defect cycle, and it is a common surprise blocker on the first full-flow lot.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to build a semiconductor fab?

A greenfield fab commonly runs 28–36 months from permits to volume production, which is roughly the span the template uses. Shell and utilities are predictable; the variance is concentrated in tool qualification and yield learning.

What is the cleanroom protocol changeover?

The point at which the building stops being a construction site and becomes a controlled environment. Gowning, airlocks, wipe-down material entry and restricted tools all begin, and trade productivity for any remaining work drops significantly.

Why is tool hook-up on the critical path rather than tool delivery?

A tool cannot be qualified until it is connected to ultrapure water, process gases, exhaust and power at spec. Delivery is a logistics event; hook-up depends on facility utilities being complete at that specific point in the fab.

When does the fab actually "open"?

At tool qualification and first silicon, not at building completion. The template treats building dry-in and cleanroom certification as intermediate gates and reserves the headline milestones for qualification and the first full-flow pilot lot.

How much contingency should I hold for yield learning?

Enough for the defect-density curve to be experimental rather than scheduled. Most programmes underestimate this phase because it is the only one where the answer is not known in advance; a separate, visible bar is better than padding the ones before it.
