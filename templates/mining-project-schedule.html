What's included

A mine is a sequence of studies before it is a construction site. The template follows the stage-gate progression the industry actually uses:

  • Exploration — Target generation, geophysics, geochemical sampling and first-pass drilling. Milestone: discovery confirmed.
  • Resource definition — Infill drilling, assay turnaround, geological modelling and a compliant resource statement. Milestone: resource declared.
  • Studies & feasibility — Scoping study, pre-feasibility and the bankable feasibility study that supports a funding decision. Milestone: investment decision.
  • Permitting & approvals — Environmental and social impact assessment, mining licence, water and tailings approvals, community agreements.
  • Development & construction — Site access, power and water, plant construction, pre-strip or shaft sinking, and mobile fleet delivery.
  • Ramp-up & operations — Commissioning, first production, ramp to nameplate capacity, plus the closure and rehabilitation plan. Milestone: commercial production.
Assay turnaround is a real schedule item, not a background task. Laboratory queues of six to twelve weeks routinely sit on the critical path between drilling and the resource model, and a project that plans drilling without planning the assay lag will report a resource months later than the chart suggests.

How to customize it

  1. Adjust the exploration and drilling phases to your deposit type — open pit and underground diverge sharply at development.
  2. Swap pre-strip for shaft sinking or decline development if the mine is underground.
  3. Extend permitting where community consultation or indigenous agreements are required.
  4. Add a tailings storage facility track; it frequently has its own permitting and construction path.
  5. Keep closure planning visible from the start — most jurisdictions require it before approval, not at the end.

Scheduling tips

  • Treat each study as a gate. Scoping, pre-feasibility and feasibility are decision points, not just deliverables — link downstream work to their approval.
  • Plan drilling around seasons. Access roads, ground conditions and wet seasons constrain rig availability far more than crew size does.
  • Order long-lead plant early. Mills, crushers and transformers have lead times that can exceed the construction window itself.
  • Ramp-up is not instant. Reaching nameplate capacity commonly takes six to eighteen months; showing it as a step change misleads everyone reading the chart.
  • Track permits individually. A single outstanding water or tailings approval can hold the whole project regardless of construction progress.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to develop a mine?

From discovery to commercial production is commonly 7–15 years. Exploration and studies dominate; construction itself is often only 2–3 years. The template compresses that lifecycle onto one chart.

What are the stage gates in a mining project?

Typically discovery, resource declaration, scoping study, pre-feasibility, bankable feasibility and the investment decision — each a gate that funds the next phase. All appear as milestones in the template.

Does this work for open pit and underground?

Both. The exploration, study and permitting phases are shared; swap the pre-strip rows for shaft sinking or decline development if your mine is underground.
