Why a research Gantt chart

A thesis is one of the longest, most self-directed projects you'll ever run — and supervisors and funding bodies almost always want to see a Gantt chart in your proposal to prove it's feasible. Beyond ticking that box, a timeline keeps a multi-month or multi-year project honest by breaking it into dated stages, so "write the thesis" becomes a set of manageable, scheduled chunks rather than a looming deadline.

What's included

  • Literature review — reading, note-taking, and drafting the review chapter. Milestone: proposal submitted.
  • Research design — methodology, instrument design, and ethics / IRB approval. Milestone: ethics approved.
  • Data collection — recruitment, fieldwork, experiments or surveys.
  • Analysis — data cleaning, analysis, and interpreting results.
  • Writing up — chapter drafts (intro, methods, results, discussion), supervisor feedback and revisions.
  • Submission — formatting, proofreading, final review and submission. Milestone: thesis submitted.

How to customize it

  1. Set your submission deadline and lay out the terms or months you have to work with.
  2. Match your methodology. Swap the generic data-collection tasks for your actual method — interviews, lab work, a survey, or archival research.
  3. Add the fixed dates your programme imposes: ethics deadlines, annual reviews, upgrade or confirmation vivas.
  4. Link dependencies so, for example, analysis can't begin until data collection is done — and a slip there shifts your write-up automatically.
  5. Export for your proposal as a PDF or image to include in your document.

Scheduling tips for researchers

  • Overlap reading and writing. Don't wait for a "finished" literature review to start writing — draft as you go, and show that overlap on the chart.
  • Build in buffer. Ethics approval, recruitment and equipment access almost always take longer than planned; add slack so one delay doesn't cascade.
  • Protect the write-up. Analysis expands to fill the time available — cap it and ring-fence enough weeks for drafting and revisions.
  • Show supervisor feedback loops. Schedule the review-and-revise cycles explicitly; they take real calendar time.
  • Zoom out for a PhD. Use a monthly or termly scale across several years and mark annual milestones so the long game stays visible.
