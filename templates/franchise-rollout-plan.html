Franchise Rollout Plan Template

A free franchise rollout plan template built around the per-unit sequence you will repeat for every site — franchisee recruitment and approval, territory mapping, site selection, fit-out, brand standards and the operations manual, training, launch marketing, opening and post-opening support. The template shows the system-level work once and then three overlapping opening waves, which is how a rollout actually runs.

What's included

A franchise rollout is not one project; it is one project template executed many times with the starts staggered. The chart separates the two:

  • System readiness — Franchise disclosure and legal pack, unit economics model, operations manual, brand standards, supplier agreements and the field support model. Milestone: system ready to sell.
  • Territory & pipeline — Territory mapping and market sizing, target unit count per market, franchisee marketing, applications, discovery days, financial qualification and approval. Milestone: first agreements signed.
  • Wave 1 units — The full per-unit sequence for the first cohort: site selection and lease, design and permits, fit-out, equipment, training, launch marketing and opening.
  • Wave 2 units — The same sequence started once wave 1 is in fit-out, so field support and construction management are not asked to be in two places at once.
  • Wave 3 units — The third cohort, sized off what waves 1 and 2 revealed about real per-unit duration and cost.
  • Support & system improvement — Post-opening field visits, performance reviews against the unit economics model, and manual and training updates fed back from what the early units learned.
The constraint is not any one opening — it is how many openings your support team can carry at once. Site selection, construction oversight, training delivery and opening-week support all draw on the same small central team, and each unit needs them for weeks. Draw the waves so those windows do not stack. A rollout that promises ten openings in a quarter with one field manager is a plan to open ten units badly.

How to customize it

  1. Set the per-unit sequence to your own real durations first, then copy it per wave — the pattern is the reusable part.
  2. Change the wave overlap to match your field support headcount; more support people means tighter stagger.
  3. Add a row per unit rather than per wave once specific sites are identified.
  4. Extend permit and licence durations by jurisdiction — the same brand takes very different times in different cities.
  5. Keep franchisee training as a hard predecessor to opening; it is the row most often compressed and the one most visible to customers.
  6. Track post-opening support visits as real scheduled work rather than as an assumed capability.

Scheduling tips

  • Do not open wave 2 sites before wave 1 has traded for a month. The first units are where you learn what the operations manual got wrong, and that learning is worth more than a faster second wave.
  • Approve franchisees slowly. A wrong franchisee is far more expensive than a delayed opening, and reversing one takes years.
  • Finish the operations manual before the first agreement. You are selling a system; if the system is not written down, every unit invents its own.
  • Sequence landlord and permit work per unit, not per programme. Each site has its own approval clock and they do not run together.
  • Book opening-week support per unit. The first trading week sets the site's habits, and remote support does not substitute for someone on the floor.

Frequently asked questions

How long does a franchise rollout take?

System readiness typically takes four to six months, then each unit runs roughly six to nine months from franchisee approval to opening. The template shows a system build plus three overlapping waves across about two years.

How many units should be in a wave?

As many as your field support team can genuinely carry through site selection, fit-out oversight, training and opening week at once — usually far fewer than the commercial plan wants. The template uses small waves for that reason.

What is the per-unit sequence?

Franchisee approval, territory and site selection, lease, design and permits, fit-out, equipment, training, launch marketing, opening and post-opening support. It repeats identically per unit, which is what makes it schedulable.

Can I use this for company-owned expansion instead?

Yes. Drop the franchisee recruitment and approval rows and keep the per-unit sequence — for a single site the retail store opening timeline is a closer fit.
