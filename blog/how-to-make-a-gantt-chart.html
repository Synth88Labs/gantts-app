How to Make a Gantt Chart: Step-by-Step Guide

By the gantts.app teamUpdated July 14, 202610 min read

To make a Gantt chart, list your tasks, give each a start date and duration, arrange them into phases, link dependencies, add milestones, assign owners and track progress, then review the critical path and export. This guide walks through all seven steps and shows how to do it free online.

What you need first

Before you draw a single bar, get three things clear. First, the goal and deadline — what "done" looks like and when it has to happen. Second, a list of tasks that gets you there. Third, a rough sense of how long each task takes and what depends on what. You do not need perfect estimates; you just need a starting point you can refine. If you are new to the format, read what a Gantt chart is first so the parts below make sense.

Step 1: List your tasks

Start by breaking the project into concrete tasks. Aim for tasks that are small enough to hand to one person and finish in a defined stretch of time — a few hours to a couple of weeks is a good range. If a "task" would take two months, split it. Write them in plain verbs: "Draft homepage copy," "Review with legal," "Deploy to production." Do not worry about order yet; just get everything out of your head and into a list. Grouping related tasks under a heading, like Design or Build, will pay off in Step 3.

A quick test for whether a task is the right size: you should be able to name one person who owns it and describe what "done" looks like in a sentence. If you cannot, the task is either too big (split it) or too vague (define it). It also helps to work from a template for your type of project so you do not miss standard tasks — browse the free template library for construction, software, marketing, and event plans you can adapt.

Step 2: Set start/end dates & durations

Give every task a start date and either an end date or a duration in working days — set one pair and the third value follows. Use working days rather than calendar days so weekends and holidays do not distort the schedule. Be honest with estimates: padding every task hides real risk, but skipping buffer entirely leaves no room for the inevitable surprises. If you are unsure, use a best-guess duration now; dependencies in Step 4 will let you adjust it later without re-entering everything.

Step 3: Order tasks & add phases/groups

Now arrange the tasks in the order they will actually happen and nest them under phase headings. A phase is a summary row — like Discovery, Design, Build, Launch — that rolls up the tasks beneath it into a single summary bar. Grouping does two things: it makes a big plan readable, and it lets you collapse detail when presenting to stakeholders while keeping it available for the team. Many tools, including gantts.app, add automatic WBS numbering (1, 1.1, 1.2) so every task has a stable reference.

Step 4: Add dependencies

Dependencies are what turn a static picture into a real schedule. Link tasks that rely on each other so that moving one shifts the others automatically. The most common relationship is finish-to-start (FS): task B cannot start until task A finishes — for example, "Test" cannot begin until "Build" is done. Three other types exist for special cases: start-to-start (two tasks kick off together), finish-to-finish (two tasks must end together), and the rare start-to-finish. FS covers the large majority of real plans, so start there and reach for the others only when a task genuinely behaves that way. Draw a link between two bars and let the tool recalculate the dates; resist the urge to hard-code dates that a dependency should control.

Dependencies also feed the critical path in Step 7 — without them, the tool has no way to know which tasks drive the deadline. If you only add one type of structure to your chart, make it dependencies.

Step 5: Add milestones

A milestone marks a significant moment with no duration — a kickoff, an approval, a delivery, or the launch itself. It appears as a diamond on the timeline rather than a bar. Milestones are worth adding for two reasons: they give stakeholders clear checkpoints to track, and they act as anchors you can link dependencies to. A good habit is to place a milestone at the end of each phase and one at the final deadline so the finish line is always visible.

Step 6: Assign owners & track % progress

Assign an owner to every task so there is one person accountable for it, and add owners to the chart so workload is visible. As work happens, update percent complete on each task. Progress shading lets you compare planned versus actual at a glance: if the "today" line has passed a bar that is still at 0%, you have slippage to address. Keeping progress current is the single biggest factor in whether a Gantt chart stays useful or becomes a stale diagram nobody trusts.

Step 7: Review the critical path & export

Finally, turn on the critical path — the longest chain of dependent tasks that determines your finish date. Any delay on a critical task delays the whole project, so these are the tasks to protect and watch most closely. Review the path, tighten or resequence where you can, and confirm the end date is realistic. When the plan looks right, export it to PDF or PNG for a report, or to Excel or PowerPoint to drop into a deck. To understand the underlying math, read the critical path method guide.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Too much detail too soon — hundreds of tiny tasks make the chart unreadable. Start at phase level and drill down only where it helps.
  • Hard-coding dates instead of dependencies — if dates do not move when a predecessor slips, the chart lies the moment reality changes.
  • No buffer anywhere — a plan with zero slack breaks on the first delay. Build realistic estimates and a little contingency near the deadline.
  • Never updating progress — an un-maintained chart is worse than none, because people trust a picture that is no longer true.
  • Ignoring the critical path — if you do not know which tasks drive the deadline, you cannot protect the deadline.
