The 3-Week Lookahead Schedule (Without Retyping It Every Monday)

Nobody builds from an eighteen-month programme. They build from the next three weeks of it. The lookahead schedule is that window — the slice of the master programme that is close enough to be real — and on most sites it is still produced by copying rows into a spreadsheet by hand, every single Monday.

19 July 2026 · gantts.app

What a lookahead actually is

A lookahead is a short, rolling extract of the master schedule — typically three weeks, sometimes two, four or six — showing only the work that touches that window. It rolls forward every week, so the same task appears in several consecutive lookaheads as it gets nearer and better understood.

It exists because a master programme and a work plan are different documents doing different jobs. The programme answers "will we finish in time"; the lookahead answers "what is happening on Tuesday, and has anyone ordered the steel". Trying to run a site meeting off a two-hundred-line programme is why people print it on A0 and then ignore it.

In Last Planner terms the lookahead is where constraints get found: the work is close enough that you can ask whether the permit, the materials, the crew and the preceding trade are all actually going to be there.

Overlap, not containment — the detail everyone gets wrong

Here is the rule that makes a lookahead correct, and the one that hand-built spreadsheets most often break.

A task belongs in the window if it overlaps the window — not if it fits inside it. The eight-month foundation package that started in March and runs to November is happening right now, and it is precisely the work the site team most needs in front of them. Filter for tasks that start and finish inside the three weeks and you will drop every long-running activity on the job, leaving a lookahead that looks tidy and describes a project nobody is working on.

The test is two comparisons, not four:

task.start ≤ window.end  AND  task.end ≥ window.start

That is the whole rule, and gantts.app applies exactly it.

Why it is anchored to Monday

The lookahead is a weekly ritual attached to a weekly meeting. A window running Thursday to Thursday would cut the working week in half and be useless to everyone reading it.

So the window always snaps back to the start of the week. Open the tool on a Wednesday and you get the window that began on Monday, not a three-week block starting mid-week. The arrows step it a week at a time when you need to look further out, and "This week" brings it back.

Generating one instead of retyping it

There is no shortage of 3-week lookahead templates — Smartsheet publishes one, so do half a dozen others, and there is even a standard format used on US Navy facilities work. Every one of them is a blank grid you fill in by hand.

That is the actual problem. The schedule already contains the answer; the weekly work is copying it out, and copying it out is where the errors and the staleness come from. Miss a task, or update the master and forget the lookahead, and the two documents quietly disagree.

In gantts.app the lookahead is a view of the same data:

  1. Set View to Lookahead.
  2. Pick the window — 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 or 8 weeks.
  3. Step it with the arrows, or press "This week" to jump back to today.
  4. Export to PDF or PNG for the wall, or Excel for the meeting.

The chart zooms to the window so three weeks are legible rather than compressed into a sliver of an eighteen-month axis. A strip across the top states which window you are looking at and how many tasks are hidden, because a filtered chart that does not say it is filtered reads as a plan with tasks missing.

Change a date in the master and the lookahead is already correct. There is nothing to re-copy.

Making it a useful document, not just a filter

  • Put a name on every line. A lookahead without an owner per task is a list of hopes. Turn on the Assignee column.
  • Break the near work down further than the far work. A task that reads "First floor fit-out — 6 weeks" is fine in the programme and useless in a lookahead. Split it as it approaches.
  • Review constraints, not just dates. The value of looking three weeks out is that there is still time to fix what is missing.
  • Keep it short. If the lookahead runs to sixty lines, either the window is too long or the plan is too granular for this meeting.

Frequently asked questions

What is a 3-week lookahead schedule?

A rolling extract of the master programme showing only the work that touches the next three weeks. It is updated weekly and used to coordinate day-to-day work and to spot constraints while there is still time to clear them.

Why three weeks?

It is long enough that constraints — materials, permits, crews — can still be resolved, and short enough that the dates are credible. Two, four and six-week windows are also common; the right length depends on your lead times.

Should a long task that started months ago appear in the lookahead?

Yes. Anything overlapping the window belongs in it. Filtering to tasks that fit entirely inside three weeks drops exactly the long-running work that is happening right now.

Is a lookahead the same as the master schedule?

No. The master schedule answers whether the project finishes on time. The lookahead answers what happens this week and who is doing it. They come from the same data but serve different meetings.

Can I make a lookahead in Excel?

Yes, and most teams do — by retyping rows from the programme every week. Generating it as a view of the schedule removes the retyping and the drift that comes with it.

How do I print the lookahead?

Switch to the Lookahead view and export to PDF or PNG. The export reflects what is on screen, so you get the window rather than the whole programme.
