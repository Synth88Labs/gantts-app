S-Curves and Earned Value: Reading Whether You Are Actually Behind

A percentage complete tells you almost nothing on its own. Sixty per cent done is excellent in week two and a catastrophe in week nine. An S-curve fixes that by plotting cumulative planned work against time, so "how far along are we" becomes "how far along were we supposed to be by now" — and the gap between the two lines is the answer.

19 July 2026 · gantts.app

Why the curve is S-shaped

Projects do not burn work at a constant rate. The opening weeks go slowly — mobilising, agreeing scope, waiting for approvals — then the middle accelerates as everything runs in parallel, then the tail slows again as the last items wait on sign-off and snagging.

Plot cumulative work against time and that shape comes out as a flattened S: shallow, steep, shallow. It is not a target anyone drew. It falls out of how work actually arrives.

Which is exactly what makes it useful as a yardstick. A straight line would imply the project should be twenty per cent done in twenty per cent of the time, and every project in history would look "behind" in its first month.

Reading planned against actual

Two curves. The planned curve comes from your baseline: spread each task's weight across its planned dates and accumulate. The actual curve comes from reported progress.

Read them vertically, at today's date:

  • Actual below planned — behind. The vertical distance is how much work you are short.
  • Actual above planned — ahead, or your progress reporting is optimistic. Both happen.
  • The curves diverging — the problem is getting worse, not stable. This is the shape that matters most and the one a single percentage never shows.

Read them horizontally instead and you get something more intuitive for a status meeting: slide left from today's actual point to where it meets the planned curve, and you have the date the plan expected you to be at this much progress. The distance is how many weeks behind you are, in the units people actually argue in.

Earned value, without the jargon

Earned value management gives the gap a number. Three quantities, and the acronyms are worse than the ideas:

  • PV (planned value, or BCWS) — the work you said you would have finished by now, valued at budget.
  • EV (earned value, or BCWP) — the work you have actually finished, valued at the same budget. A task worth £10,000 that is 40% done has earned £4,000. What it cost you is irrelevant here, deliberately.
  • AC (actual cost, or ACWP) — what you have really spent.

From those three:

  • SV = EV − PV — schedule variance. Negative means behind.
  • CV = EV − AC — cost variance. Negative means over budget.
  • SPI = EV ÷ PV — below 1.0 means behind. An SPI of 0.85 means you are getting 85p of progress for every pound of plan.
  • CPI = EV ÷ AC — below 1.0 means over budget.

The separation is the whole point. A project can be perfectly on budget and badly late, or on time and haemorrhaging money, and one blended "percentage complete" hides both.

Why our tool refuses to show you a CPI sometimes

This is worth stating plainly, because most free EVM calculators do the opposite.

Actual cost is the one input that cannot be derived from a schedule. It has to come from your accounts. The tempting shortcut is to assume a task that is 40% done has consumed 40% of its budget — and if you do that, AC equals EV by construction, so CPI comes out as exactly 1.00 for every project that has ever existed. The number looks authoritative, responds to nothing, and would tell someone with a runaway budget that they are perfectly on track.

So gantts.app leaves the cost metrics blank until you enter a real "Spent" figure on your tasks. Schedule metrics — SV and SPI — still work, because those need only dates and progress. A missing number is honest. A confident wrong one is not.

For the same reason, the earned curve behind today is reconstructed rather than recorded: the tool stores your progress as it is now, not a history of every past reading. It is exact at today, where the numbers are read, and approximate behind it — and it says so on the panel rather than in a footnote.

You do not need a budget to get a curve

Most plans carry no cost data at all, and an S-curve that needs one is an S-curve nobody draws.

If no task has a cost, gantts.app weights each task by its working-day duration instead. The shape is the same and the axis reads in percent rather than currency — a pure progress S-curve. Add costs later and the same panel becomes a value curve without you changing anything else.

One thing does matter though: set a baseline. Without one, "planned" can only mean your current dates, and your current dates already include every slip that has happened. Schedule variance will read as zero forever, which is a very reassuring and completely useless answer. Baseline ▸ Set baseline, once, at the point the plan is agreed.

How to build one here

  1. Build or import your schedule and get the dates roughly right.
  2. Baseline ▸ Set baseline when the plan is agreed. This freezes what "planned" means.
  3. Optionally give tasks a Budget in the task panel, so the curve reads in money rather than days.
  4. Update % complete as work happens — this is what the earned curve is made of.
  5. Optionally enter Spent per task to unlock CPI, cost variance and the forecast at completion.
  6. Click 📈 S-curve.

Frequently asked questions

What is an S-curve in project management?

A graph of cumulative planned work against time. It is S-shaped because projects start slowly, accelerate in the middle and taper at the end. Plotted against actual progress, the vertical gap between the two curves is how far ahead or behind you are.

What is the difference between an S-curve and earned value?

The S-curve is the picture; earned value is the arithmetic behind it. EVM puts numbers on the gap — SV, SPI, CV and CPI — where the curve shows you its shape and direction.

What does an SPI of 0.9 mean?

You have earned 90% of the value the plan said you would have by now, so you are roughly 10% behind schedule. Below 1.0 is behind, above 1.0 is ahead.

Do I need cost data to use an S-curve?

No. Without costs, tasks are weighted by working-day duration and you get a progress curve — the same shape, read in percent. Costs turn it into a value curve and unlock the cost metrics.

Why is CPI not showing?

Because no actual costs have been entered. CPI needs real spend; deriving it from percentage complete would make it exactly 1.00 for every project. Add a Spent figure to your tasks and it appears.

Can I make an S-curve in Excel?

Yes, and most people do — it means maintaining a cumulative column and a chart by hand, and re-doing it whenever dates move. Generating it from the schedule removes that step.
