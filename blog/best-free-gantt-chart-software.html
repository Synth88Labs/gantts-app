What "free" really means (free vs freemium vs trial)

Before comparing tools, it helps to separate three very different business models that all get marketed as "free":

  • Free — no cost, no expiry, no feature held hostage. Open-source apps and genuinely free web tools fall here.
  • Freemium — a limited plan that's free forever, but caps something (projects, tasks, collaborators) or paywalls key features like export or the critical path. You can use it for real work only if you stay inside the caps.
  • Free trial — full access for a fixed window (often 14–30 days), after which you must pay to keep using it. Useful for evaluation, not a long-term free solution.

All three are legitimate. The problem is only when a trial or a tightly-capped freemium plan is presented as simply "free." Below, we label each tool by which model it actually uses.

The 9 tools, reviewed honestly

1. gantts.app — free, no login

Full disclosure: this is our tool. It's a browser-based Gantt maker that's fully free with no sign up or login, no task or project caps, automatic critical path, dependencies, milestones, and one-click export to PDF, PNG, Excel and PowerPoint. Your data stays in your browser. The trade-off is that it's focused on Gantt planning and export rather than being a full work-management suite with time tracking, chat and resource billing — if you need those, a heavier platform may suit you better. Best for: anyone who wants a real Gantt chart fast, free, without an account.

2. GanttProject — open-source desktop

GanttProject is a mature, free, open-source desktop application (Windows, macOS, Linux). It supports tasks, dependencies, the critical path, resource assignment, and export to PDF, PNG and CSV. Because it's a download rather than a web app, it works offline and there's no account — but the interface feels dated and there's no built-in cloud sync. Best for: users who prefer installed software and open-source licensing.

3. OnlineGantt & other small free web tools

A handful of lightweight web tools (such as OnlineGantt and similar single-purpose sites) let you sketch a Gantt chart in the browser and export an image or PDF. They're handy for a quick one-off chart and some don't require an account, but feature depth varies widely — many lack a reliable critical path, robust dependencies, or spreadsheet export. Best for: fast, disposable charts. Verify each tool's current limits before relying on it.

4. TeamGantt — capable, but capped free plan

TeamGantt is a polished, collaboration-focused Gantt tool. Its free plan is usable but limited — typically around one project and roughly 60 tasks for a small number of users, with the critical path and richer export reserved for paid tiers. It requires an account. Best for: small teams evaluating a paid collaboration tool who fit within the free caps.

5. GanttPRO — trial-based

GanttPRO is a well-designed commercial Gantt tool with strong scheduling, workload and collaboration features. It offers a free trial rather than a free plan, so it's paid once the trial ends, and it requires an account. Best for: teams ready to buy who want to trial a dedicated Gantt platform first.

6. Instagantt — freemium

Instagantt is a Gantt tool known for its Asana integration and clean timelines. It's freemium: a limited free tier exists, but many features (advanced export, critical path, more projects) sit on paid plans, and it requires an account. Best for: Asana users who want a connected timeline view.

7. Canva — design-first

Canva can produce a nice-looking Gantt graphic from its templates, exportable to PNG or PDF on the free tier (account required). But it's a design tool, not a scheduling engine — there are no real dependencies, no automatic rescheduling, and no critical path; the bars are shapes you place by hand. Best for: a polished, static Gantt visual for a slide or report where accuracy of scheduling isn't the point.

8. Microsoft Project — powerful but paid

Microsoft Project is the enterprise standard for serious scheduling, with deep dependency logic, resource leveling, baselines and critical path. It has no free plan — it's subscription or license-based and requires a Microsoft account. Best for: large organizations with complex programs and the budget to match.

9. Excel & Google Sheets — the DIY option

You already have a spreadsheet, and you can bend it into a Gantt chart with a stacked bar chart or the Sheets Timeline view. It's free if you own the software, but there are no dependencies, no critical path, and lots of manual upkeep. See our Excel guide and Google Sheets guide. Best for: tiny, static plans where the data already lives in a sheet.

Free tools that need no sign up

If avoiding an account is a priority — for privacy, speed, or just to test an idea — the field narrows quickly. The options that let you build a Gantt chart without creating an account are:

  • gantts.app — open the site and start immediately; no account, and your plan is stored in your own browser. Includes critical path and full export.
  • GanttProject — no account, but you download and install a desktop app rather than working in the browser.
  • Some small web tools — a few let you export without signing up, though features are basic; confirm before you rely on one.

Most of the polished web platforms — TeamGantt, GanttPRO, Instagantt, Canva — require an account before you can do anything. That's a reasonable choice for them; it's just worth knowing which side of the line each tool sits on.

How to choose

Match the tool to what you actually need:

  • Just need a Gantt chart fast, free, with export? Use gantts.app — no account, critical path and Excel/PPT export included.
  • Prefer installed, offline, open-source software? GanttProject.
  • Building toward a paid team platform and fit the free caps? TeamGantt or Instagantt free tiers.
  • Enterprise program management with resource leveling? Microsoft Project or GanttPRO (paid).
  • Only need a static picture for a slide? Canva, or export a PNG from gantts.app.

Whatever you pick, decide up front whether you need dependencies, a critical path, and real export — those three are exactly where "free" plans most often disappoint. If they matter, start with a tool that includes them for free. New to the concept? Read what is a Gantt chart, then try building one, or browse free templates to start from a ready-made plan.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best free Gantt chart software?

It depends on your needs. gantts.app is the best fully-free, no-login web option and includes export and critical path. GanttProject is the best free open-source desktop app. TeamGantt and Instagantt have capable free tiers but cap projects or tasks and require an account.

Which Gantt chart tools are actually free, not just a free trial?

gantts.app (fully free, no account) and GanttProject (open-source, free to download) are genuinely free. TeamGantt and Instagantt offer limited free plans. GanttPRO is trial-based, and Microsoft Project is paid — these are not free in the long run.

Is there a free Gantt chart tool with no sign up?

Yes. gantts.app requires no account, sign up or login — you open the site and start building immediately in your browser. Most other web-based Gantt tools require you to create an account before you can use them.

What's the difference between free, freemium and free trial?

Free means no cost with no expiry. Freemium gives a limited plan free forever but charges for higher limits or key features. A free trial gives full access for a short window, after which you must pay. Many tools marketed as free are actually freemium or trial-based.

Which free Gantt chart tools include the critical path?

gantts.app includes automatic critical path for free, and GanttProject supports it on the desktop. In several commercial tools the critical path is reserved for paid tiers, so check before relying on it.
