Gantt chart definition

A Gantt chart is a type of bar chart that illustrates a project schedule. It maps a list of activities against a horizontal time axis so you can see, in a single view, the start date, end date, and duration of every task in a plan. Because the bars sit on a shared calendar, a Gantt chart also reveals which tasks run in parallel, which must happen in sequence, and how the whole project fits together from kickoff to finish.

In project management terms, a Gantt chart is a scheduling tool. It answers four practical questions at once: what has to be done (the task list), when each thing happens (the bars on the timeline), in what order (dependencies between tasks), and how it is going (progress shading). That combination is why the format has survived for more than a century and remains the default view in nearly every project planning tool today.

What does a Gantt chart look like?

Picture a spreadsheet grid. The left-hand columns hold your task names and details. To the right, the columns become a calendar — days, weeks, or months. For each task, a colored bar stretches across the cells that correspond to its scheduled dates. A short task is a short bar; a task that spans three weeks is a bar three weeks wide. Read top to bottom and you see every piece of work; read left to right and you see the flow of time.

Parts of a Gantt chart

Almost every Gantt chart is built from the same six elements:

  • Task list — the rows down the left side naming each activity, often grouped into phases or a work breakdown structure (WBS), sometimes with columns for start date, end date, duration, and owner.
  • Timeline (date axis) — the calendar running across the top that sets the horizontal scale, whether in days, weeks, quarters, or a mix.
  • Task bars — the horizontal bars themselves. Position shows when a task happens; length shows how long it takes.
  • Milestones — key moments with no duration, such as a sign-off or launch, usually drawn as a diamond rather than a bar.
  • Dependencies — arrows connecting bars to show that one task must start or finish before another can begin.
  • Progress — shading or a bar-within-a-bar showing percent complete, so you can compare planned versus actual at a glance.

Many charts add a vertical "today" line, a highlighted critical path, and resource or owner labels, but the six parts above are the essentials.

What is a Gantt chart used for?

Gantt charts are used anywhere work has to happen in a coordinated order against a deadline. Typical uses include:

  • Planning a project schedule — laying out every task, its duration, and the sequence from start to finish.
  • Coordinating a team — showing who owns what and when, so people can see how their work connects to everyone else's.
  • Managing dependencies — making it obvious that, say, testing cannot start until the build is done.
  • Tracking progress — comparing planned dates with actual progress to spot slippage early.
  • Communicating with stakeholders — giving clients, sponsors, or executives a one-page picture of the plan and its status.
  • Resource and deadline management — checking that the same person is not booked on two tasks at once, and that the end date is realistic.

You will find Gantt charts in construction, software development, marketing, event planning, manufacturing, consulting, research, and product launches — essentially any field where a project has a beginning, an end, and steps in between.

Gantt chart examples by project type

The format stays the same, but the tasks change with the domain. A few concrete examples:

  • Software development — rows for discovery, design, sprint 1, sprint 2, QA testing, and release, with a milestone diamond at go-live and a dependency from QA to release. See the software project template.
  • Construction — site prep, foundation, framing, roofing, electrical, plumbing, and inspection, heavily dependency-driven because each phase depends on the previous one finishing.

Advantages and disadvantages

Gantt charts are popular for good reasons, but they are not the right tool for every situation. An honest look at both sides:

Advantages

  • Clarity at a glance — the whole schedule fits on one visual, which is far easier to grasp than a table of dates.
  • Shows dependencies — you can see how a delay in one task ripples through the rest of the plan.
  • Reveals overlap and workload — parallel bars make it obvious when too much is scheduled at the same time.
  • Great for communication — non-experts understand a Gantt chart immediately, making it ideal for stakeholder updates.
  • Supports the critical path — it pairs naturally with critical path analysis to find the tasks that truly drive the deadline.

Disadvantages

  • Can get complex — a project with hundreds of tasks produces a very tall, hard-to-read chart.
  • Needs maintenance — if reality changes and no one updates the chart, it quickly becomes misleading.
  • Doesn't show effort or cost directly — bar length is duration, not hours of work or budget, which can be misread.
  • Less suited to highly iterative work — teams doing continuous, unpredictable work often prefer a Kanban board.

The practical fix for most of the downsides is a tool that keeps the chart in sync automatically when you change a date or dependency, rather than a static picture you redraw by hand.

Who invented the Gantt chart?

The chart is named after Henry L. Gantt (1861–1919), an American mechanical engineer and management consultant who developed and popularized this style of scheduling chart around 1910 to 1915. Gantt's charts were used to plan production and, famously, wartime shipbuilding, and they helped establish visual scheduling as a management discipline.

Gantt was not strictly the first, though. In the 1890s, Polish engineer Karol Adamiecki designed a very similar chart he called a harmonogram, but his work was published mostly in Polish and Russian and did not reach a wide audience. Because Gantt's version spread through American and European industry, the format took his name — which is why we still call it a Gantt chart today.

Gantt chart vs timeline vs Kanban

These three tools are often confused, but each does a different job:

  • Gantt chart — a scheduling view with tasks, durations, dependencies, owners, and progress. Best when order and timing matter and you need to manage a plan over time.
  • Timeline — a simpler sequence of dated events on a single line, usually with no durations or dependencies. Best for illustrating milestones or history at a high level. Every Gantt chart contains a timeline, but a timeline is not a full Gantt chart.
  • Kanban board — columns like To do, In progress, and Done, with cards moving left to right as work flows. Best for continuous, flexible work where what happens next matters more than on which date. Kanban shows status, not a calendar.

A useful rule of thumb: use a Gantt chart when there is a deadline and a defined sequence, a Kanban board when work flows continuously without fixed dates, and a plain timeline when you only need to show a handful of key dates.

Frequently asked questions

What is a Gantt chart in simple terms?

A Gantt chart is a horizontal bar chart that shows a project schedule over time. Tasks are listed down the left side and a calendar runs across the top, with each task drawn as a bar positioned to show when it starts, when it ends, and how long it takes.

What is the main purpose of a Gantt chart?

The main purpose is to plan and communicate a schedule: it shows what needs to be done, in what order, by when, and who is responsible. It makes overlaps, dependencies, and the overall timeline easy to see at a glance.

Who invented the Gantt chart?

The chart is named after Henry L. Gantt, an American mechanical engineer and management consultant who popularized it around 1910 to 1915. An earlier similar chart was designed by Polish engineer Karol Adamiecki in the 1890s, which he called a harmonogram.

What are the main parts of a Gantt chart?

The core parts are a task list, a timeline or date axis, horizontal task bars, milestones shown as diamonds, dependency arrows linking related tasks, and progress shading that shows percent complete.

What is the difference between a Gantt chart and a timeline?

A timeline is a simple sequence of dated events on one line. A Gantt chart is richer: it shows many parallel tasks with durations, dependencies, owners, and progress, so it can actually be used to manage the work, not just illustrate key dates.
