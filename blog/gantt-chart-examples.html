1. Construction project

A construction Gantt chart is heavily phased and dependency-driven: pre-construction (permits, design, site survey), site prep & foundation, structure & framing, MEP and interiors, and inspection & handover. Tasks chain tightly — you can't pour footings before excavation, or drywall before rough-in inspection passes — so finish-to-start dependencies and the critical path do real work here. Key milestones include permit approval, foundation sign-off, "dried in," and final inspection.

Because so much work is sequential, a construction chart is where the critical path earns its keep — it shows which single delay (a late permit, a failed inspection) would push the whole completion date, so the site manager knows exactly what to protect. 2. Software / agile development

A software Gantt chart maps releases and sprints rather than a single linear build: discovery, design, then repeating sprint blocks for build and QA, followed by UAT and release. Typical tasks are epics broken into stories, code review, testing, and a launch checklist. Milestones mark sprint reviews, feature-complete, code freeze and go-live. Even agile teams use a Gantt view to communicate a release timeline to stakeholders who live outside the backlog.

The trick with a software Gantt chart is altitude: keep the timeline at the epic and sprint level for stakeholders, and let the day-to-day live in your issue tracker. A two-week sprint becomes one grouped bar with a review milestone at its end, not thirty individual story bars. See the software development template, or open a blank plan in the editor.

3. Marketing campaign

A marketing campaign Gantt chart coordinates several tracks running in parallel: strategy & planning, content production, design & assets, channel setup (email, paid, social), and launch & reporting. Tasks like drafting copy, approvals, ad creative and scheduling overlap across teams, so swimlane-style grouping by workstream keeps it legible. Milestones include creative sign-off, campaign launch, and the post-mortem.

4. Event planning

An event Gantt chart counts down to a fixed, immovable date, which makes backward scheduling essential. Phases run venue & budget, vendors & logistics, marketing & registration, run-of-show prep, and event day & wrap-up. Typical tasks: book venue, confirm caterer, send invites, finalize agenda, rehearse. Milestones include contracts signed, registration open, and the event itself as a hard deadline milestone.

Because the end date can't move, event planners often build the chart right to left: start from the event date and work backward, letting each task's deadline fall out of the dependency chain. That immediately reveals whether there's enough runway — if working backward pushes "book venue" into last week, you know you're already behind. 5. Product launch

A product-launch Gantt chart aligns product, marketing, sales and support around one go-live date. Phases: readiness (final QA, pricing, docs), go-to-market (messaging, landing pages, PR), enablement (sales and support training), and launch & post-launch. Cross-team dependencies are everywhere, so the critical path shows exactly which slip would move the launch. Milestones: feature freeze, launch date, and the 30-day review.

6. Research / thesis timeline

A research or thesis Gantt chart spans months and is milestone-heavy: literature review, proposal, data collection, analysis, and writing & revision. Tasks tend to be long and sequential, with dependencies on approvals (ethics board, supervisor sign-off) that are easy to underestimate. Milestones include proposal defense, data-collection complete, first draft, and submission. It's a favorite for students because it turns a vague year into a visible plan.

7. Small business project

Small-business Gantt charts are lean and practical — opening a shop, launching a service, or running a quarterly initiative. Phases might be plan, set up, launch, and review, with a handful of tasks each. The value here is less about complex dependencies and more about a shared, at-a-glance schedule that a small team can actually keep updated. Milestones: financing secured, doors open, first sale.

8. Simple weekly plan

Not every Gantt chart spans months. A weekly plan zooms the axis to days and lays out tasks across a single week — useful for content calendars, sprint weeks, or personal productivity. Phases collapse into simple groupings (Mon–Fri), tasks are short, and dependencies are light. It's the friendliest on-ramp for anyone new to Gantt charts.

Whatever the domain, the structure repeats: group tasks into phases, connect the ones that depend on each other, mark the moments that matter as milestones, and let the critical path tell you where the risk is. Notice how different the axes are — a construction job spans months on a weekly grid, a launch runs on a daily grid over a few weeks, and a weekly plan zooms all the way to hours. The Gantt chart flexes to all of them; what changes is the timescale and the density, not the underlying idea.

What makes a good Gantt chart

Across all 12 examples, the strong charts share the same traits:

  • Clear phases. Group related tasks so the plan reads as a story, not a wall of bars.
  • Real dependencies. Link tasks that must happen in order so a slip in one automatically pushes the rest.
  • Visible milestones. Mark approvals, launches and deadlines as distinct points people can rally around.
  • A highlighted critical path. Know which tasks actually drive the finish date, so you protect the right ones.
  • Readable density. Keep roughly 10–30 bars per view; roll big projects up into phase summaries and break detail into sub-charts.
  • Status at a glance. A today line and % complete turn the chart into a live report, not a static plan.

Just as important is what to leave out: a Gantt chart is a communication tool, not a task database. Resist the urge to log every sub-task, and push granular detail down into a checklist or issue tracker so the chart stays scannable in a single glance. New to this? Frequently asked questions

What are some common examples of Gantt charts?

Common Gantt chart examples include construction schedules, software and agile release plans, marketing campaign timelines, event plans, product launches, research or thesis timelines, small-business projects, and simple weekly plans. Each lays tasks along a time axis with phases, milestones and dependencies.

What does a good Gantt chart look like?

A good Gantt chart groups tasks into clear phases, shows dependencies between related work, marks milestones as distinct points, highlights the critical path, and stays readable — usually 8 to 20 visible bars with short labels and a today marker for status.

How many tasks should a Gantt chart have?

For a readable chart, aim for roughly 10 to 30 tasks grouped into phases. Larger projects are better handled by rolling detail up into summary bars per phase, then breaking each phase into its own detailed chart.
